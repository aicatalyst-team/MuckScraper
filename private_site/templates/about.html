MuckScraper — About This Paper

The News Should Inform You.
Not Consume You.

A different approach to staying informed — slower, deeper, and free from the algorithm.

The 24-hour news cycle was not designed to inform you. It was designed to keep you watching. Every breaking alert, every live update, every "developing story" is optimized for engagement — not understanding. By the time the facts settle, you've already moved on to the next thing. MuckScraper was built as a quiet rebellion against that.

Two editions a day. Twenty stories each. That's it. No feed, no scroll, no algorithm pulling you deeper in.

Instead of a live stream, MuckScraper publishes two editions daily — morning and evening — each with twenty stories that earned their place. The pipeline watches hundreds of sources throughout the day, groups articles about the same event, ranks them by importance, and produces a single cohesive summary for each edition. By the time you open it, the noise has already been filtered out.

Get out of the echo chamber

Every story on MuckScraper comes with something most news apps deliberately withhold: a view of how other outlets are covering the same event. When you read about a policy decision, you can see how a center-left paper framed it alongside how a center-right one did. The bias ratings — sourced from AllSides where available, and AI-assessed otherwise — are there not to tell you who to trust, but to remind you that every story is told from somewhere.

The goal is not to tell you what to think. It's to give you enough room to think for yourself — which is something the current media environment actively works against.

How it works, under the hood

MuckScraper is powered by a pipeline built entirely on local, self-hosted infrastructure. It fetches from NewsAPI, GNews, and a curated list of RSS feeds, groups articles using vector embeddings, and generates summaries and deep analytical reports using Ollama — a local large language model runner. No cloud APIs. No subscription fees. No data leaving your machine.

The backend is an open-source project, freely available to anyone who wants to run their own independent newsroom from their own hardware.

MuckScraper is a personal independent project, and still a work in progress. The grouping, summaries, and edition workflow are being refined over time, with the aim of building a more transparent, story-first way to follow the news rather than a finished black-box product.

Questions, corrections, source recommendations, or suggestions for the paper are welcome at editor-in-chief@muckscraper.news.
